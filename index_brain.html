Understanding Brain Stroke
Risk Parameters

This guide explains each clinical and lifestyle metric used by the AI model to evaluate your risk of a brain stroke. Understanding these factors can help you and your doctor make proactive, life-saving decisions.

Demographics
Age
Single strongest stroke predictor

Stroke risk doubles every decade after age 55. Aging weakens arterial walls, promotes plaque buildup (atherosclerosis), and increases the likelihood of atrial fibrillation — a major stroke trigger.

Lower risk: <45 Moderate: 45–65 High risk: 65+
Gender
Biological sex at birth

Men face higher stroke risk at younger ages due to lifestyle patterns and hormonal differences. However, women account for more total stroke deaths because they live longer.

Ever Married
Marital status as a health proxy

Married individuals tend to have better chronic disease management, reduced stress hormones, and faster emergency response times during stroke events.

Residence Type
Urban vs. rural living environment

Urban dwellers face more pollution and sedentary stress risks. Rural populations may have limited access to emergency stroke care — and in acute stroke, time-to-treatment is critical.

Work Type
Occupational category and stress profile

Work type is a proxy for occupational stress, physical activity levels, and socioeconomic status. Private sector workers show higher stroke rates. Occupational stress raises cortisol levels, increasing blood pressure and clot-forming activity.

Children — Very low risk group Govt — Moderate stress Private / Self-employed — Higher stress
Medical History
Hypertension
High blood pressure diagnosis

Hypertension is the single most important modifiable risk factor for stroke, responsible for roughly 54% of all stroke cases. Sustained high pressure weakens artery walls and can directly rupture blood vessels in the brain.

No Hypertension Hypertensive — Critical risk
Heart Disease
Pre-existing cardiac condition

Heart disease — particularly atrial fibrillation — dramatically raises stroke risk. Clots that form in the heart can travel to the brain. People with AF are 5× more likely to have a stroke.

No Heart Disease Heart Disease — 5x elevated risk
Lab & Lifestyle
Average Glucose Level
Blood sugar level (mg/dL)

Elevated blood glucose accelerates atherosclerosis and promotes clot formation. Levels above 126 mg/dL indicate diabetes.

Normal: 70–99 Pre-diabetic: 100–125 Diabetic: 126+
BMI
Body Mass Index

Obesity raises stroke risk by promoting hypertension, diabetes, sleep apnea, and atrial fibrillation. Even modest weight reduction significantly reduces risk.

Normal: 18.5–24.9 Overweight: 25–29.9 Obese: 30+
Smoking Status
Tobacco use history and current status

Smoking doubles the risk of ischemic stroke by promoting atherosclerosis, raising blood pressure, and increasing blood viscosity. Quitting is the single most impactful lifestyle change for stroke prevention.

Never Smoked — Lowest risk Formerly Smoked — Reduced but elevated Currently Smokes — 2x stroke risk

High-Risk Combinations to Watch

  • Hypertension + Heart Disease
  • Age 65+ + Diabetes
  • High Glucose + Smoking
  • Obesity (BMI 30+) + Hypertension
  • Heart Disease + High Glucose
  • Smoking + Hypertension + Age 60+

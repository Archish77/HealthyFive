Understanding Kidney Disease
Risk Parameters

This guide explains each clinical and lifestyle metric used by the AI model to evaluate your risk of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD).

Demographics
Age
Primary demographic risk factor

The risk of kidney disease increases significantly with age. After 60, the kidneys naturally lose about 1% of their filtering ability each year.

Low risk: <40 Moderate: 40–65 Higher risk: 65+
Gender
Biological sex at birth

Men have a slightly higher baseline risk due to hormonal differences and lifestyle patterns. However, diabetes-related CKD can affect women more severely.

Lifestyle Factors
Physical Activity Level
Frequency & intensity of exercise

Regular physical activity improves blood pressure, reduces obesity, and improves insulin sensitivity — all of which directly protect the kidneys.

Low — Sedentary Moderate High — Regular exercise
Sitting Hours per Day
Total sedentary time daily

Prolonged sitting independently raises risk of hypertension and metabolic syndrome even in physically active individuals.

Ideal: <6 hrs Concern: >8 hrs
Sleep Hours
Average nightly sleep duration

Both too little (<6 hrs) and too much (>9 hrs) sleep are associated with impaired kidney function and higher CKD risk.

Optimal: 7–8 hrs Risk: <6 or >9 hrs
Smoking
Tobacco use

Smoking reduces blood flow to the kidneys by damaging blood vessels and doubles the speed of kidney function decline in people with diabetes or hypertension.

Alcohol Consumption
Regular alcohol intake

Chronic heavy alcohol use raises blood pressure, causes dehydration, and forces the kidneys to filter excess toxins. It also interacts negatively with medications used to manage kidney disease.

Diet & Hydration
Water Intake (Liters/day)
Daily fluid consumption

Adequate hydration keeps kidneys flushed and prevents crystallization of waste products. 2.5–3.5L is typically ideal for healthy adults.

Risk: <1.5L Optimal: 2.5–3.5L
Urination Frequency
Number of times urinating per day

Normal urination frequency is 6–8 times per day. Very low or very high frequency can indicate dehydration, diabetes, UTI, or early kidney dysfunction.

Normal: 6–8 times Abnormal: <4 or >10
Salt Intake Level
Dietary sodium consumption

High sodium causes the kidneys to retain water, raising blood pressure and forcing the glomeruli to work under higher pressure — gradually destroying them over time.

Low — Protective High — Harmful
Protein Intake
Dietary protein consumption level

Excessive protein generates high levels of nitrogen waste that the kidneys must filter. In people with existing kidney issues, high protein diets accelerate kidney function decline.

Normal — Standard diet High — Elevated strain
Medical History
Blood Pressure Level
Systemic blood pressure status

Hypertension is the second leading cause of kidney failure worldwide. High blood pressure damages the small blood vessels in the kidneys, reducing their filtering ability.

Normal (<120/80 mmHg) High (≥130/80 mmHg)
Diabetes
Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes diagnosis

Diabetes is the single leading cause of kidney failure globally, responsible for roughly 40% of all cases. High blood sugar damages the glomeruli over years (diabetic nephropathy).

No Diabetes — Lower risk Diabetic — Major risk factor

⚠️ High-Risk Combinations to Watch

  • Diabetes + High BP
  • High salt + Low water
  • Smoking + Alcohol
  • Sedentary + High sitting hours
  • Age 60+ + Diabetes
  • High protein + Low water

Understanding Liver Disease

Demographics
Age
Primary demographic risk factor

Liver disease risk accumulates over time. The liver's regenerative capacity declines after age 50.

Lower risk: <40 Moderate: 40–65 Higher risk: 65+
Gender
Biological sex at birth

Men have higher prevalence of liver disease due to higher alcohol consumption rates. However, women are more susceptible to alcohol-induced liver damage at lower doses.

BMI (Body Mass Index)
Weight-to-height ratio indicator

BMI is a strong predictor of Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD). Excess fat deposits directly in liver cells, causing inflammation and eventually fibrosis or cirrhosis.

Normal: 18.5–24.9 Overweight: 25–29.9 Obese: 30+
Lifestyle Factors
Alcohol Consumption
Weekly alcohol intake in standard units

Alcohol is directly hepatotoxic and metabolized almost entirely in the liver. Chronic heavy use causes fatty liver → alcoholic hepatitis → cirrhosis → liver failure.

Safe: <7 units/week High risk: >14
Smoking
Current tobacco use status

Smoking exacerbates liver disease by increasing oxidative stress and inflammation, and is strongly associated with hepatocellular carcinoma (liver cancer).

Physical Activity
Weekly hours of exercise

Regular physical activity directly reduces hepatic fat content, improves insulin sensitivity, and lowers inflammation. Even 150 minutes/week of moderate activity can reduce liver fat significantly in NAFLD patients.

Sedentary: <1.5 hrs/week Active: >3 hrs/week
Genetic & Medical History
Genetic Risk
Hereditary predisposition level (0–2)

Genetic variants like PNPLA3 significantly increase susceptibility to fatty liver disease. Individuals with first-degree relatives who had liver disease carry markedly higher baseline risk.

Low (0) — Minimal hereditary risk High (2) — Strong family history
Diabetes
Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes diagnosis

Insulin resistance drives excess fat accumulation in liver cells. Diabetics are 2–3× more likely to develop advanced liver fibrosis and are at elevated risk for liver cancer.

No Diabetes — Lower risk Diabetic — Elevated risk
Hypertension
High blood pressure diagnosis

Hypertension damages portal blood vessels leading to the liver and is closely linked with NAFLD. The combination of hypertension, diabetes, and obesity is a "triple threat" for liver disease.

Normal (<120/80 mmHg) Hypertensive (≥130/80 mmHg)
Lab Results
Liver Function Test (LFT) Score
Composite marker of hepatic health

The LFT score incorporates measures like ALT, AST, bilirubin, albumin, and ALP. Elevated scores indicate hepatocellular damage or impaired synthetic function. This is the model's most direct biochemical indicator.

Healthy: <35 Borderline: 35–50 Elevated: >50

⚠️ High-Risk Combinations to Watch

  • Diabetes + High BMI
  • Alcohol + Smoking
  • High Genetic Risk + Alcohol
  • Hypertension + Diabetes
  • Sedentary + Obese (BMI 30+)
  • Elevated LFT + Alcohol Use

Demographics
Age
Primary demographic risk factor

Lung cancer risk increases significantly with age. Most diagnoses occur after age 65, as decades of cellular exposure to carcinogens accumulate.

Lower risk: <45 Moderate: 45–65 Higher risk: 65+
Gender
Biological sex at birth

Men have historically had higher lung cancer rates, but the gender gap is narrowing. Women exposed to smoking may develop the disease at lower cumulative tobacco doses.

Lifestyle Factors
Smoking
Tobacco smoking status

Smoking is responsible for approximately 85% of all lung cancer cases. Cigarette smoke contains over 70 known carcinogens that directly damage DNA in lung cells.

Non-smoker — Low baseline risk Smoker — Dominant risk factor
Alcohol Consuming
Regular alcohol intake

Chronic alcohol use compounds lung cancer risk, especially in smokers, by impairing the body's ability to metabolize and detoxify cancer-causing chemicals.

Peer Pressure
Social influence on smoking behaviour

Peer pressure is a documented gateway to smoking initiation. Individuals who began smoking due to social influences tend to have longer smoking histories.

Yellow Fingers
Nicotine staining on fingertips

Yellow fingers are a physical marker of heavy, long-term tobacco use and indicate chronic high-volume smoking — a direct proxy for cumulative carcinogen exposure.

Absent — No staining Present — Heavy tobacco use
Symptoms & Medical Indicators
Anxiety
Presence of anxiety symptoms

Chronic respiratory conditions cause oxygen deficiency and breathlessness that triggers anxiety responses. It is also associated with smoking as a coping behaviour.

Chronic Disease
Presence of existing chronic illness

Pre-existing conditions like COPD and chronic bronchitis dramatically elevate lung cancer risk, creating an inflamed, damaged lung microenvironment susceptible to malignant transformation.

None — Lower risk Present — Elevated risk
Fatigue
Persistent unexplained tiredness

Unexplained persistent fatigue is one of the most common early warning signs of lung cancer. Tumors consume significant metabolic energy and the immune response causes systemic exhaustion.

Allergy
Presence of allergic reactions

Allergies can cause chronic airway inflammation that shares pathological features with early-stage lung disease. Certain environmental allergens may also be directly carcinogenic.

Wheezing
Audible whistling sound during breathing

Wheezing indicates airway narrowing or obstruction. In a lung cancer context, it can result from a tumor partially blocking a bronchus — a red-flag symptom in smokers or older adults.

Absent — Normal airway Present — Possible obstruction
Coughing
Persistent or chronic cough

A chronic cough — especially one that changes in character or produces blood-tinged sputum — is the most common presenting symptom of lung cancer. The model weighs persistent coughing heavily.

Persistent — Investigate urgently
Shortness of Breath
Dyspnea — difficulty breathing

Dyspnea arises from tumor compression of airways, pleural effusion, or reduced lung function from cancer invasion. It is a significant symptom at both early and advanced stages.

Swallowing Difficulty
Dysphagia — trouble swallowing

Can indicate that a lung tumor has grown large enough to compress the esophagus, or that cancer has spread to mediastinal lymph nodes.

Chest Pain
Persistent or pleuritic chest discomfort

Chest pain in lung cancer may be dull and persistent from tumor growth involving the chest wall, or sharp and worsened by breathing (pleuritic). Combined with coughing, shortness of breath, and a smoking history, it is one of the highest-risk symptom combinations the model evaluates.

Absent — Lower suspicion Present — High-priority flag

⚠️ High-Risk Combinations to Watch

  • Smoking + Yellow Fingers
  • Coughing + Chest Pain
  • Shortness of Breath + Wheezing
  • Chronic Disease + Smoking
  • Fatigue + Swallowing Difficulty
  • Alcohol + Smoking + Age 65+
